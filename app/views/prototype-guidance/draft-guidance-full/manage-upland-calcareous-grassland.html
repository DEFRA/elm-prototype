Guidance

U19: Manage upland calcareous grassland

Use this guidance if you manage rough grazing or moorland on limestone in Northern England. The wildflowers growing in these areas prefer soils rich in the calcium and magnesium found in limestone. Careful grazing management will maintain these limestone grassland habitats. Hardy upland livestock keep coarser grass in check, conserving some of rarest plants in England. If you are interested in creating limestone grassland refer to Guidance note: creating species rich grassland.

From: Natural England

Where can you find limestone grassland in the uplands?

You may find limestone grassland:

  • in the North Pennines, Yorkshire Dales, Cumbria and North Lancashire
  • on shallow soil where limestone or lime-rich rocks are close to the surface
  • associated with limestone pavements and outcrops, scars and screes
  • across extensive areas of open fell especially in the Yorkshire Dales
  • alongside upland heath, blanket bog, upland acid grassland and scrub and woodland

What is limestone grassland in the uplands?

Limestone grassland is often intermingled with other types of grassland. Where limestone is close to the surface look for:

  • blue moor-grass, its prominent blue flowers often dominating the view in the spring
  • the typical wildflowers of limestone grassland such as dog violet, thyme, rock-rose, fairy flax, harebell and bird’s-foot trefoil

Why managing limestone grassland in the uplands is important

Limestone grassland in the uplands provides nutritional year-round grazing for livestock. Extensive grazing management will:

  • maintain some of England’s largest areas of flower-rich grassland
  • favour plants and insects adapted to the combination of open grassland, thin soils and harsh climate
  • provide breeding ground for birds such as skylark and curlew which need a mix of long and short grass
  • protect archaeology beneath the ground, find further information in Historic features in grassland

How do you know when you are successful?

All year

If grazing is right you will see:

  • patches of short grass the size of a table top separated by areas grazed to ankle height
  • flowering sedges and fine-leaved grasses
  • at least one third of the vegetation as wildflowers rather than grasses
  • exposed rock or bare soil about the size of a hoofprint where insects may be basking on sunny days
  • clumps of scrub providing shelter for birds, insects and spiders

Spring

If grazing is right you will see:

  • early flowering plants such as blue moor-grass, primrose, dog violet, and early purple orchid
  • prominent pink flowers of bird’s-eye primrose in damper areas

Summer

If grazing is right you will see:

  • the attractive heads of quaking grass trembling in the wind
  • at least four typical wildflowers in a patch about one metre square in front of your feet, most obvious may be common rock-rose, birds-foot trefoil, wild thyme, harebell and tormentil

Autumn

If grazing is right you will see:

  • late flowering plants such as the purple globes of devil’s bit scabious
  • longer vegetation in areas of rocky scree, pavement or ledges

Considerations for successful management

Look at the following issues to help achieve successful management of limestone grassland in the uplands.

Grazing

Your judgement about livestock numbers through the year is crucial to get the best from limestone grassland. Find further information in Guidance note: Grassland and livestock grazing

What to look out for

You may need to remove livestock when:

  • the height of the grass sward is less than 3cms
  • you see bare ground larger than the size of a hoofprint
  • your livestock need routine supplementary energy feed

Grazing levels may need increasing or changing when:

  • the height of the grass sward is over 15cms
  • large-leaved grasses dominate
  • there is more than 1cm of dead plant litter during the growing season

Tips for success

You can consider:

  • using small hardy breeds of cattle, their grazing creates a mix of long and short grass
  • moving sheep elsewhere for four to six weeks during summer to help plants to flower and set seed
  • using sheep to graze down grass in late autumn and winter

Tree and scrub management

Encourage the growth of scattered clumps of trees and scrub. These will help to attract birds such as black grouse which enjoy a mix of habitats. Where trees and scrub are spreading on to limestone grassland, you may need to increase grazing levels or remove trees and scrub by hand. This will help to conserve the typical wildflowers in the grassland. Find further information in Guidance note: management of scrub and trees.

Sowing wildflowers

Introduce typical wildflowers to boost their numbers. For best results, scatter seed collected from nearby limestone grassland. Scarify before this to create bare ground, helping seeds to germinate. Use the weight of seed to work out how much to scarify. You can also plant plugs grown from seed directly into the ground. This works well for plants which are difficult to germinate.

Find further information in Guidance note: increasing flowers in grassland.

Manure, fertiliser and lime

You should not spread manure or fertiliser. Adding nutrients will help competitive grasses to grow. Typical wildflowers will be lost.

Supplementary feeding

Avoid creating bare ground and weedy areas caused by supplementary feeding of livestock. Routine use of energy feed suggests that livestock numbers are too high. Move the location of mineral feed sites each time you put feed out.

Managing limestone grassland for insects, reptiles and butterflies

Insects, reptiles and butterflies need a mix of habitats to complete their life cycle. You can help by managing limestone grassland to have:

  • flowers which provide nectar for food
  • grass at a range of heights for shelter, food and nesting
  • fine-leaved grasses and wildflowers, especially foodplants for caterpillars of butterflies such as small heath and northern brown argus
  • some scrub for shelter, food and nesting
  • small areas of bare ground for nesting, burrowing and basking

Further information is available in Guidance note: managing grassland for insects reptiles and butterflies.

Managing undesirable plants

Control species such as creeping thistle, spear thistle, bracken, tall rushes and broad-leaved dock. This will provide space for wildflowers to grow and livestock to graze. Find further information in Guidance notes: Controlling undesirable species and Managing bracken and Managing rush.

Legal requirements

You will need to:

  • obtain consent from the Environment Agency if you need to use herbicides near rivers and streams
  • consult Natural England if altering grazing levels, sowing or planting within a Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI)
  • consult Historic England if undertaking certain activities on a Scheduled Monument
