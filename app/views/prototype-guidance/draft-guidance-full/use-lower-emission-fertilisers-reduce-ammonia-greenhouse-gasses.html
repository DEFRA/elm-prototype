The effects of ammonia emissions

Ammonia is released when nitrogen from fertilisers, slurries and manures comes into contact with air. When mixed with other air pollutants, such as diesel fumes, ammonia forms tiny toxic particles which lead to:

  • deadly health problems such as cardiovascular and respiratory disease
  • poor air quality
  • soil and water acidification
  • the growth of rough grasses and nettles which stop other species growing

Agriculture accounts for 88% of UK ammonia emissions.

Benefits of using lower emission fertilisers

When using urea-based fertilisers, up to 45% of nitrogen can be lost as ammonia, depending on conditions such as weather and soil type. In comparison, when using ammonium nitrate (AN) fertilisers, typically up to 2% of nitrogen is lost as ammonia.

Switching from urea-based fertiliser to AN fertiliser can reduce your ammonia emissions and the harmful effects they have.

While AN can be more expensive, the net cost difference may be negligible due to lower nitrogen losses.

How to use lower emission fertilisers

All applications of manufactured nitrogen fertilisers should:

  • be based on a nutrient management plan
  • integrate fertiliser and manure supply
  • take into account your soil management plan

You should regularly maintain, calibrate and test all application equipment.

Only apply AN fertiliser:

  • according to crop requirement
  • in cool and moist conditions

Do not use AN fertiliser when rainfall is expected as rain can increase nitrogen loss.
